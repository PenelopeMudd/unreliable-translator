The Edge of Y

Yesterday, Scott and I took the kids to Monster Jam. For those not from the Midwest — or any place where loud, bouncy trucks don’t routinely fling dirt while flying through the air — it’s a “contest” where flashy, huge-tired dragons do flips like 12,000-lb. (per the in-Jam trivia) truck-beasts.

Now, anyone who has ridden with me on an escapade already knows: my natural habitat is a road that barely qualifies as one. Cow path? Perfect. Two ruts in the dirt? Even better. And I do it with the windows up and the A/C on. That’s why it’s fun. What can I say? I’m a limit-tester. It’s what I do.

When I’m on a motorized adventure, I’m a big fan of passengers nervously asking, “Are we allowed to drive here?” Eyebrows migrate toward the nose-ridge as the eager, yet unsure, rider turns to look toward the driver’s seat — while I am, of course, already driving in said questionable place. My inner monologue: We can until we get caught, and I don’t see anyone around with enforcement energy, so…

Which brings me, in the roundabout way my stories always do, back to Monster Jam, and one certain reddish farm truck that had the misfortune of sitting with the keys in it a bit too regularly, while a restless preteen with far too little supervision roamed wild.

My love of moving fast in vehicles has a direct genetic line from the Wilson side. I can still hear Dad explaining how to take those curves without tapping the brakes — easing in, then hitting the gas halfway through the arc. He imparted this knowledge like a sacred technique. That was living. Soaring over back roads with Jim, Jimmy, James D. Wilson at the wheel. “Mountain Music” playing from Alabama’s repertoire. Hair in my face, body lifting off the seat as we launched to the crests of the Hills of Y.

The safest place — the closest place to the Sun — was us smiling, him talking, telling stories, singing songs. Flying in an old Chevy. Being a Wilson in the center of the world: Near Ware or Dittmer, Missouri, Jefferson County, USA. Back before I knew the world could be more than finicky, I lived, if not in, very close to a state of confusion — in the naming sense if nothing else.

“Where do you live?”

Ware.

“Where?”

Ware. On Y.

“Why, no, I mean where?”

I know, but there isn’t much there to explain. There is a church on the top of the hill.

Yes, I know. It’s confusing. We live near Ware, Missouri, on this Highway — well, Road; “highway” is being generous — Y, like the letter before Z. Mailing address is Dittmer. Physically we’re between 21 and 30. Yes, really. Nevermind. Just meet me at the church.

In this truck, amid the geographical and historical confusion that was the Wilson Farm, Hidden Valley Acres — yes, hidden on the edge of Y. That seems appropriate. Rarely, when the moon was just right, this truck was usually the place I got His attention without having to share with the rest of the worlds. All of it. Enough of it. Every single drop I could wring from the moment. I doubt it ever would’ve been enough, but I would’ve liked to try to see if saturation was even theoretically plausible.

The emotional geometry of it all — how closeness tilts the memory, how longing bends the light, how the story shifts depending on where you stand.
Kim Starkey · 2025
