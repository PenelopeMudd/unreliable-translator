Say Her Name Again

Today, I saw a video from an educator I admire about the murder of Renee Nicole Good in Minnesota, and it sent me back to a place I hadn’t considered for a long time, St. Paul, where a little girl played very convincingly at being capable and grown. The one walking alone on the streets of downtown St. Paul, trying to get to shelter before dark. From the summer of 1990 when I was 17, alone in St. Paul, and pretending adulthood was something you could bluff your way through if you walked fast enough, read enough books, and didn’t look lost.

At this time, my baby was 25½ weeks old in an incubator at United Children’s. I had $500, a Missouri diploma, a boy who didn’t stay, and a city that didn’t particularly care whether I survived or not— or so I thought— as I walked past the soup kitchens on the way to see Jordan Louise, fighting for her life like she would every single day as an infant, and later as a Momma.

While Jor was busy learning to breathe unassisted, I was walking back and forth to the dormitory the hospital’s social worker helped me find. In this case, shelter was a downtown college-type home that mostly served the Deaf Community — similar to a Ronald McDonald House.

After baby daddy left, I lived in this residence totally alone for about a month, I think. The details are fuzzy as they are wont to be. However, what I do remember was Nurse Stephanie, much too serious to be Stefanie, but kind, warm, and truly gifted in both NICU Nursing and Caring.

Then, there was another nurse. I can’t remember her name, but she reminded me of every Minnesota mom who would invite you in for a good meal, dontcha know? And would ask me if I wanted to go with. With who/whom? When/Where? I mean, I might have been a single 17 year old mom, but I also loved appropriate grammar, so this mis/use of prepositions fascinated me. When she invited me to dinner and boardgames, I went eagerly; it was one of the best nights of that whole summer.

The other nurse I remember most, I’m sure I have glamorized her somewhat, but she looks like a kind of Nurse Cameron Diaz. To me, even in nursing scrubs, she fairly sparkled. Somehow, the nurses found out that my 18th birthday was coming up. This Nurse “Diaz” took me out to lunch and to the make-up counter.

She told me that we would go to Clinique because it was “young girl’s make-up.” She was an Estee Lauder girl, but she couldn’t have been very old, I think. Maybe, kindness makes one glow. I’m not sure, but these women felt safe. Completely professional and capable, but also decidedly human. Nothing they did for me was ever “rewarded” in any of the ways we currently measure, but they helped a baby and her baby survive that summer, not quite so alone.

That’s what I thought about when I saw Joe Beckman’s video. I thought about Nurse Stephanie, the Minnesota Mom, and Nurse Diaz. I wondered if they still lived in the area, or if they had family in the area. I wondered if Mrs. Good was related to them in any way, but I mean aren’t we all, to some extent, so I’m sure that this tragedy is affecting those women in some ways with an intensity I’m immune from here in Missouri.

Then, I thought of the shooter, who was a career officer in many branches of service. In June 2025, he was involved in a serious on-the-job incident. I do not know this man, but I do know some of the circumstances that make working in high-stress situations difficult. I also know that the reactions we hope we would have versus the one we actually have are not always the same.

I haven’t looked further into him because I’m not particularly interested in him as a person. I think he was part of a systemic structure that forgot even strong, tough, capable, well-trained service members are human with human reactions to compounding traumas and vicarious traumas. If it was not this man and Renee Nicole Good on this day, it could have been a dozen other men who said that they were, “fit for duty” because to say otherwise would be at the best weakness or even cowardice to his fellow team members.

Truthfully, when I saw the video, and I saw her wheels turn right, I knew exactly what her mind thought, “Oh my god, there is a gun, and my wife is in this car.”

She chose escape because that is what women are taught to do, and her nervous system was on high alert, screaming

— Fight, Flight, Freeze, or Fawn? —

on repeat to the rhythm of her previous life-altering experiences.

We— humans— are hard wired for survival. Her system shifted to FLEE in response to the tune of her basest instincts. Unfortunately, his hard wiring was connected to training, lethality, and a gun when his nervous system alerted to FIGHT.

This is where the two stories meet: a woman fleeing and a man firing, each acting from the systems that built them. Much like a little girl with a baby in a big city.

Nothing bad happened to me while walking the streets of St. Paul that summer— not because it couldn’t have— it just didn’t. That was a fortunate season in many ways, but now when I think about the women I met at United Children’s and Renee Nicole Good, her wife, and her family, I feel emptied of anything useful. Because from here in Missouri, what else can we possibly do besides Say Her Name, Again?

In honor of all Creatures harmed by authoritarianism,

Kim Starkey, PhD, unreliable translator
