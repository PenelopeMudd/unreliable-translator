The Platner Problem

If you’ve been even marginally following national politics, you may have seen the story of Graham Platner. A blunt-spoken, gravelly-voiced Marine veteran from Maine, Platner launched his campaign in a dirty hoodie on his boat, shucking oysters and talking straight about the “oligarchy,” billionaires, and the need for working-class politics. To many, he came across as the rare candidate who felt like a regular guy who had actually lived the struggles he was naming.

On July 6, Platner’s campaign encountered a testimony that they couldn’t morally explain away. A woman who previously dated Platner publicly alleged that in 2021 he entered her home while drunk and forced her to have sex despite her telling him to stop. Platner denied the allegation. Prominent Democratic supporters quickly withdrew their endorsements.

This latest political fall from grace made me think of a recent text exchange with a friend about Bill Clinton testifying before Congress. I said I was glad Clinton finally went on record about his connections to Epstein.

My friend replied, “And Bill Clinton is a pig though.”

Initially, I was frustrated. I thought, “Can’t we celebrate ONE win for women in this CF-of-a-world right now?”

Alas, upon reflection, my friend was correct, and I’m also — unfortunately — right, in a gut-punch-to-the-self sort of way. Clinton’s behavior was opportunistic and predatory, and to me it seems completely expected for the time. With that uninvited bit of self-awareness whirring like a gnat in my brain, I began to reflect on why I might start defending Bill Clinton simply because I do not think he was/is a pedo. cringe

We begin this reflection in the late 80s, where an older schoolmate and I went out driving after an impromptu party. Before too long, we parked in a dark lot away from traffic. As kids in cars did then and I assume still do — despite many parents’ beliefs — the boy and I became “friendlier.” After a bit, he suggested the process continue past where I was comfortable, and I told him no, thanks anyway. Unimpressed by my lack of consent, he told me I could then walk home. When I started to get out of the car, he took it back, looked annoyed that he’d wasted his time, and silently drove me home. We never spoke again.

Living as a young adult through the 1990s, I remember how common the lecherous behavior of older men was. This was a time often where outings were laced with alcohol and boundary-crossing was minimized or laughed off. Today we minimize the effects of the past. We often profess ourselves to “know better now”; we may wish all more enlightened on the role of Power in relationships, but humans are creatures of culture and habit. Change is reluctant — especially when the people who assumed a mantle of personal moral certainty have never considered their retrospective impact on others.

As a rape crisis volunteer, a therapist, and a middle school educator, I have spent much of my life believing women and girls. In my 20s, I visited hospital rooms late at night where I supported many girls by holding their hands as police questioned or Sexual Assault Nurse Examiners collected evidence for a trial that was years distant at best. Later, other volunteers and I attended trials when possible and offered support for the survivor’s understanding of a legal process they never asked to be involved in.

Sitting with these thoughts — the news story, my friend’s text, a long-forgotten parking lot, and the hands of girls and women I’d sat beside in hospital rooms — I realized I wasn’t really thinking about Graham Platner anymore. I was thinking about myself.

Why was I instinctively defending Bill Clinton? Not because I think he is “innocent,” and certainly not because I approve of the abuse of power. If anything, I’ve always questioned people who want too much control. Perhaps it’s because, having lived through the gray areas of the 80s and 90s, my brain tried to map that messy, lived human complexity onto a man I only know through a screen. We want our villains to be entirely monstrous and our heroes to be entirely pure, because it makes judging them easier.

Which brings me back to how we empower our community members to be thoughtful consumers of political information. These days, we ask ordinary people to make extraordinary moral judgments about the character of people we’ve never actually known. We do it with headlines, testimony, campaign ads, social media, and fragments of lives we mistake for whole people. Sometimes we’re right. Sometimes we’re wrong. Most of the time, we can’t really know.

So that’s where I’ll leave this for now, and I hand it to YOU! Yes, you, reader, near and far from Arnold, Mo:

In a world where we’re expected to make profound moral judgments about people we’ve never actually known, what kind of evidence should count?

If the answer isn’t found in Washington, where should we begin looking instead?
