Warner Day 1: Sunday, June 14

When we last left our grateful—yet skeptical—grandmother, she was headed out to the airport for her grandson’s Make A Wish Trip to Disney, made possible through the Warners’ First Things First Foundation.

Sunday, June 14 — Welcome Dinner at Epcot

  • Daytime: Universal Studios or SeaWorld — do NOT use Disney ticket today
  • 6:30 PM — Meet at Epcot front entrance, by the flag pole (allow time for parking/tram)
  • 6:45 PM — Welcome dinner
  • 7:45–8:15 PM — Individual family photos with Kurt, Brenda, Mickey & Minnie
  • 9:00 PM — Epcot Luminous Fireworks

If you have ever tried to orchestrate a family trip, you know that there are many ways to do this — some more likely to succeed than others. Now, adjust your frame for “success” to include five or so families of individuals who have at least one member each with complex medical needs, and add a dose of theme park Florida sun in June. Then, mix in your own seven adult children and their respective family members for a party of around 40–50 people on the move. At minimum, you will need an itinerary, and after twenty-four years of practice, First Things First had this part down.

Following the day’s schedule, close to 6:30 pm, our family of five — nametags in place — approached a group clustered outside the gates of Epcot. In our party was Avery, seven-year-old-cancer-butt-kicker; his mom, Jordan; his dad, Randy; Finley, Avery’s little sister and self-appointed Princess; and me, Mimi, aka Jordan’s mom.

Not wanting to be those people who welcomed ourselves to the wrong group, we warily circled a bit hoping to see someone who “looked like a Warner.” Soon, we were greeted by a volunteer, and once all families were accounted for at the flagpole, approximately thirty of us were patiently escorted to a banquet room inside Epcot for dinner and greetings.

The moment we passed through the entry-way buffet, my daughter and I began to scan, working to put faces to names of the six Warner children in attendance this year. From the introductory letters from Jennifer Zink, the organization’s Executive Director, the party would also include three of the Warners’ own grandchildren, so we worked to discern who was a “Wish Kid” like Avery and who was a Warner. Near the front to the left, we saw Kurt welcoming families. Off to the right we saw Brenda near Zack Warner, the Warners’ oldest son and inspiration for the yearly Disney expedition.

Before too long, we were offered drinks, and once all families were accounted for, Kurt called everyone’s attention to the front of the room. He welcomed everyone and thanked them for sharing this trip. Next, he had each volunteer line up along the front of the room to introduce themselves and share their favorite Disney ride. At this point, it solidified something our family had begun to infer: all volunteers were either Warner kids or friends and family of those children. After all had individually introduced themselves, Brenda began to speak.

Ms. Brenda, as the kids would come to call her, introduced Zack, sharing with the room how life had changed after his accident and how this trip — now in its 24th year — grew out of their faith and their family’s devotion to Zack’s happiness and potential in the world. As she spoke, Brenda kept one hand near Zack’s arm while the other gently patted his chest. The only word I can think of to describe her look was pure adoration. It is a maternal gaze. To see it is to feel it.

After this warm introduction, all were encouraged to visit the buffet-style pizza dinner that awaited. As we began to eat, Kurt and Brenda wandered through the room, welcoming individuals and their families, but always focusing on the kids in the group. Each circled and sat next to the Wish Kids — both one at a time and together — asking about their interests and thanking the families for being there.

Soon Kurt and Brenda were lined up with Mickey and Minnie Mouse for pictures. As Kurt picked up kids and smiled for photos, volunteers circled and checked to see that every family’s needs were met and beverages were filled. All the while, volunteers and families began to gather around various tables to get acquainted.

Before too long, with the food portion nearly complete and pictures with Mickey and Minnie wrapping up, an impromptu game of chase began to wind throughout the room — kids from all tables interacting and screeching throughout the area with nary a care in the world, including who might be “it,” a questionable role that no child seemed overly concerned about defining. This impromptu playground included a Warner grandbaby who crawled under and around kids, unfazed to be at her first such unlikely party of recent strangers amid a banquet in Epcot.

With the eating and the greeting wrapping up, we met our next group of Disney Hosts and began to experience what it means to travel with VIP Disney Access. As our combined group — now closer to fifty people — meandered across Epcot, every family was accompanied by their very own volunteer to attend to each family’s needs.

Our volunteer, Sierra Warner, brought over a stroller for Finley and was soon attempting — at Finley’s direction — to hold hands with her as they walked through the park. Although Sierra gave it her best, Avery, rarely one to enjoy being singled out, stayed closer to his mom and dad — for now. Once we reached a cordoned-off space near the water across from the evening show, chairs arrived, followed by cold water and frozen treats to balance the exceptionally hot Florida evening. As all gathered to watch the show, our volunteer checked in to see that each popsicle that fell was replaced and napkins were provided for those kids — and adults — who didn’t always hit their mouths.

Finally, after the fireworks, Kurt called everyone together to go over the events for Monday. With Sierra’s phone number for direct contact, we headed back to Give Kids the World Village in a haze of Disney Magic, First Things First style — feeling in awe of the day and convinced that our family did indeed have the “best Warner” of all the volunteers.
