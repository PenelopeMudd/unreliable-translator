The Last Bundle of Other People’s Laundry

On sorting unmentionables that never belonged to me.

I’ve realized something that should have been obvious all along:

  1. People who love you don’t poke at your tender spots.
  2. I should be first among those people who treat me with kindness.

You see, I’ve spent too many years shuffling around the dirty piles of others’ opinions of me — cluttering up my mind and soul. Now, this mess isn’t the result of a choice I made to live life dodging bunches of undies I did not soil. However, the congestion began with pointed and off-hand proclamations, settling into my body before I had the language to refuse.

This was from an era when people spoke to me with cruelty, and I had few skills to dispute their assessments. Once upon a time, when I was still young enough to hope someone might want to protect me — even though, in that same dark place, I never believed anyone would.

Today, I’m done with that logic. From here on, I banish others’ uninsightful, decontextualized judgments from my mind, where they should never have been introduced in the first place.

Unfortunately, back in the day, when a partner told me, “You’re good looking, and everything, but I’m the only one who thinks so,” I assumed he was probably right. I didn’t notice anyone disagreeing with him. Nobody bothered to whisper in my ear that those who love you don’t actively work to diminish your self-confidence.

Alas, I likely would not have listened anyway. Were they trying to dissuade me, they would have needed to speak authoritatively enough to drown, “You’re nothing but a whore,” and “What did you think would happen?” Methinks there’s not enough rain in Oklahoma — to quote Carrie Underwood.

In my defense, those people who offloaded opinions seemed convinced I was the proper receptacle for their crusty viewpoints, and being sure of almost nothing, I mostly agreed. The reflections they made of my life were the only data I knew to analyze sometimes. Those images festered in the mind of this young, unwed mother with more heart than sense on lonely nights.

Now, I know those weren’t truths. They were apparitions dressed up in hand-me-down shame. Fortunately, I don’t accept that type of vocabulary in my wardrobe anymore — not even as jewelry.

Today I evict others’ razor-sharp words flung pedantically from privilege and the “way it’s always been done.”

Also, while the world is sideways or upright, I will listen to my psychosomatic systems. For example, when I notice my thumbs tucked in and my shoulders crawling toward my ears — my body is telling me it’s time to breathe or back away.

At the time when I absorbed others’ curses, I didn’t even know my system had other settings I could choose. These days, I work to listen and to respond kindly to what my body tells me. With that wisdom, I can say:

Kim Wilson didn’t deserve those words,
the judgment they wore,
nor the hand-me-down disdain stitched with her name.

Neither does Kim Starkey —
the mom, the friend,
the hopeful one, the PhD,
the unreliable translator speaking in an authentic voice
no matter who listens.
Kim Starkey · 2025
