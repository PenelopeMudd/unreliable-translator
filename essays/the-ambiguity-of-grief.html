The Ambiguity of Grief

The letter I never knew I needed

March 2025

Dear Kim Marie,

It’s getting close. I know it and so do you. You’re going to be tough; go at it like you’re “killing snakes.” Unfortunately, before you put my passing behind you, I fear you will visit with some demons wearing yesterday’s worries — or maybe you’ll just need to stifle the last gasps of old ghouls. While I can’t predict your exact path through grief, I can speak from experience as I wish, even now, my mom was here beside me to face cancer.

I know you thought I’d be bossing you around forever — even though I’m not bossy — but I’m going to have to let others worry and giggle about your best schemes and nonsense. Now, don’t start reminding me that I’d never follow my own advice, but you need to depend on your family and friends. They have offered their love and support for a reason. I know we all could use some work on our vulnerability, but you’re going to have to lead by example. Good luck with that, Doctor! It’s going to be awful, and you’ll wish it was over, but it won’t be for a ridiculously, horribly long time.

Someday, when you think you’ll just continue to wallow and luxuriate in your orphanhood, you will, in the words of so many authors, “release the breath you didn’t know you were holding,” and when you do, and if you are the most careful at paying attention, you will find that you feel a little bit better — not too much, not all at once, but slowly, eventually you will notice a lack of weight, a smidgen less dread, a kitty-cat’s eyelash of gloom will drift off into the breeze as you sit with your friends, your family, your flowers, your pup, your thankfulness. In those moments you will smile because you will remember me and will know that I love you so very much and am so very proud of you.

Love always,

Mom

Yep, Momma, it’s really hard to let go of our moms. I love you, too.
Kim Starkey · 2025
