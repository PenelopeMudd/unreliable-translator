Seeking the Misaligned

Dear Gentle Reader,

Here’s what this is not: it is not the introduction of my “best year ever.” It is not to say I’m starting a non-profit, or even raising awareness for feline disobedience. Consequently, I’m not interested in talking with people who are just fine—everything’s fine, I mean, what can ya do? Why bother? It’s fine. Instead, I’m searching for those of you who may not know exactly what you are, but are positive it’s not fine.

To be clear, this letter is a beacon for people who hobble through life with a bit of a metaphorical pebble in their shoe. Or maybe yours is that gnat that refuses to leave your ear because “something here isn’t right, and I’m pretty sure it’s not me this time.” If I’ve found you—or you’ve found yourself here—we’ll likely have one thing in common: we’re tired of being reasonable inside unreasonable systems.

While cartwheeling through one of the strangest years of my life, I’ve found myself thinking about systems—educational systems, political systems, family systems, and my obligations to, and impacts on, the systems I inhabit. In that process, I’ve come up with a lot of whys.

Specifically: Why do people feel trapped in systems, as if they have no agency? Why don’t we design systems for humans? Why don’t we authentically ask children about the quality of their education?

Okay, that one is a bit specific—but really, if you want a ground-level, eagle-eyed point of view, ask a kid. Perhaps we’re worried that what they describe will be bigger than the adult listening can endure.

Soapbox: tucked back in, slightly.

Tussling among these whys has led me to consider how incompetent systems stifle the emotional and physical health of the people they were created to support. In unhealthy systems, inconsistency breeds mistrust, gossip fills the gaps, and positions that are already demanding become toxic. When systems are broken, humans suffer needlessly, and prolonged periods of distress take a real toll on our health.

As a public school teacher for the last eighteen years—and a family member for fifty-three—I’ve learned both too much and not enough about the world from these systemic afflictions. What I know for certain is this: in systems without psychological safety, truth doesn’t disappear; it goes underground. That suppression creates tension, which then reinforces the same frustrating cycles in how people interact with the system—and with each other.

For example, people say yes when what they actually mean is that they don’t have the bandwidth to sincerely engage. They want those naming tensions to stop pointing out fires that feel unquenchable. Later, when similar issues resurface, leaders become frustrated, believing a plan was made—without realizing that what felt like agreement was often silence under pressure. I see this pattern across many organizations, but I know it most intimately from within educational systems.

Contemplating what to do in a universe hell-bent on uncertainty and broken systems, I’ve been thinking about the ways I’ve been complicit in allowing systems to marginalize my needs—and the needs of the students I serve. With that discomfort in mind, I’ve returned to many of the lessons from my work in human-centered design (HCD).

This approach to problem-solving emphasizes rapid ideation, creative confidence, and deep user empathy. Early in the process, the goal is to get as many reasonable ideas out in the open as possible—letting them mingle, bump into one another, and occasionally frolic around together. When resonant ideas are given room to find each other, innovation has a chance to emerge. Just as importantly, these moments of authentic interaction allow us to encounter new ways of thinking and to question our own assumptions.

Having an honest picture of how humans actually work within a system is essential to designing solutions that last. If we aren’t willing to surface—or even acknowledge—certain tensions, we risk creating little more than band-aids rather than sustainable, preventative responses to systemic dysregulation.

While I have little power individually to recalibrate unwieldy systems, this drive to question their inefficiencies has forced me to realize that continuing to be reasonable inside unreasonable systems isn’t neutral—it’s participatory. When I stay silent and smile, I abandon my obligation to the systems I’m in.

If I can do that and still feel satisfied within a system, then perhaps that’s acceptable. But when that silence becomes detrimental to my well-being, I can no longer carry it. This is my acknowledgment that while I may not be able to change others, I can hold boundaries with myself around what I will and will not accept. As Elphaba reminds us, “Some things may never change, but until I try I’ll never know.”

All I really want is the opportunity to launch an intention—a small nudge from my end of the universe to yours. I want us to talk, in person and tech-assisted, about our own personal tensions and how we might ease them. I want to scheme wild plans to make the world better with people who say Yes, And instead of No, But. Where do I find my people—those who feel misaligned with the systems they inhabit—in this shared quest for generative homeostasis?

With hope and possibility,

Kim
