Darmok at the End

There’s an episode of Star Trek: The Next Generation where Picard, the most handsome older man alive, meets a race of beings who only speak in metaphor. As anyone who has tried to learn a different language knows, it’s really difficult to manage the myriad ways one can use figurative language with little to no knowledge of the language’s cultural context. For example, if I told you that my two male friends were giving me “Bert and Ernie” vibes, I might mean they were two bachelors living together. However, if I focused on the musical Avenue Q’s rendition, it might mean that at least one of the characters was gay and trying to stay snugly in the closet. What I’m getting at is, this mode of communication can make it very difficult to try to speak with any depth or nuance in the overall imparted message unless your recipient is fluent in the metaphors of the culture.

Why, you may ask, am I regaling you with stories of metaphors from a space travel show of the 90’s? Well, over the last six weeks, my 95 year-old mother was under hospice care due to cancer in her bladder. That part is sad, but not especially tragic because Mom lived a rewarding, full life for which she was thankful. In short, she felt mentally prepared to leave this plane and adventure off into the great beyond, but her body, still following the previous 95 years of instinct, clung to life “like it was killing snakes,” which is the simile she used to approach any mundane or arduous task.

With the tone of this “death business” being set by Mom and her reptile-inspired mode of attacking a problem, I expressed some concerns:

Me: I’m worried about how we’re managing your pain level.

Mom: Well, that’s going to do you a lot of good.

Needless to say, the woman who thrived on her independence for all of the previous years was telling me that we would pry her autonomy, in the words of Charlton Heston, out of her “cold, dead, hands.” Unfortunately, balancing her need to “do it [her]self” with her caretakers’ needs for her to express her vulnerability so we could truly understand and honor what her body was telling us about facing its demise, became a somewhat murky prospect.

Although I’m sure that you can see how caring for someone whose programming defaults to strict self-reliance might be a challenge, I took on the task gladly. Typically, my reptile-inspired mode in crisis is: Head down; move forward. However, what I failed to envision was that staying up night shifts while supporting my mother’s care would send me full-speed ahead into a bipolar manic swing. This first, truly epic, mental peak I faced in my adult life decided at this most inconvenient time, to say Tah dah— with all the fanfare. This resulted in my communicating as fast and furiously as I could to get all possible words out before I forgot them. Uh oh.

Here’s a bit what it felt like: you’re getting ready to ride the Batman roller coaster, but before you get on the ride, someone comes to you with a clearly important, possibly life-changing group of words. Soon, you’re trying to both remember the message and find the recipient as the cart glides and flips you through the air. At best, the message will not be appropriately received, and more confusion will ensue. At worst, you may get enough of your ideas out, but with the rapid-fire delivery, and the loss of your train of thought those around will begin to question your grip on reality.

While I’ve always been a rollercoaster fan, it’s not what I would’ve chosen as my mom was on hospice care. Alas, my brain was driving the planes, trains, and automobiles, and it was taking no prisoners. Between dropping thoughts and raging trajectories, this adventure caused some serious turbulence in my communications. Fortunately, my long-time safety net, Kelly, came to help work as a buffer for, and interpreter of, me. I find it remarkable that nobody else could have handled the situation the way she did. It turns out close to 40 years of context gave her the tools to help me, and her support gave me the courage to confront the thoughts attacking my sound reasoning processes and attempt to operate with, rather than against, them.

To the strains of her beloved New Orleans Jazz, Momma’s hospice story ended like all should: her final breaths diminished in frequency and strength with loved ones around her. Thankfully, my story continued successfully as well, although I’m not sure all who encountered me would say so. I’m happy to report that my safety net worked because I trusted Kelly implicitly to take care of me; she believed me and loved me even when I said I was Britney with the clippers in her hand, or we would be Thelma and Louise with brakes. Finally, I told her that I was “The Awakening” without the swim. She didn’t get that one because it’s a kind of niche literary reference, but she gets me, and in this case, that was enough. I love you, Kelly, and I’ll never forget the gift you gave me and my family.
